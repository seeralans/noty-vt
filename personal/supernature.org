* Abstract

- Contemporary physics achieves extraordinary predictive and explanatory success across virtually all observable scales, from subatomic particles to cosmological structures.
- This success has led many philosophers and scientists to adopt, often implicitly, the thesis of explanatory closure of the physical: the view that every event in principle admits a complete physical explanation.
- The present paper challenges this metaphysical thesis on formal grounds.
- By treating physical explanation as derivability from the axioms of physical law conjoined with initial conditions, we frame physical theory as a formal explanatory system.
- We then apply Gödelian incompleteness reasoning to show that any such system, if consistent, cannot be complete.
- Consequently, there must exist true event-statements that lie outside physical derivation.
- These non-derivable events provide logical space for what may properly be termed supernatural events.
- Crucially, the argument does not assert that any particular supernatural event has occurred, nor does it characterize what such events would look like.
- It establishes only that science cannot rule out supernatural events in principle, thereby undermining a common argument against the possibility of miracles.

* Introduction

** The Success of Physical Science

- Physics has achieved remarkable success in describing, predicting, and manipulating natural phenomena.
- Newtonian mechanics accurately describes the motion of medium-sized objects at non-relativistic speeds.
- Electromagnetism unifies electric and magnetic phenomena and predicts the existence and properties of electromagnetic radiation.
- Quantum mechanics predicts atomic spectra, chemical bonding, and semiconductor behaviour with extraordinary precision.
- General relativity describes gravitational phenomena from planetary orbits to black holes and gravitational waves.
- The Standard Model of particle physics accounts for three of the four fundamental forces and has been confirmed to remarkable accuracy.
- These achievements are genuine and should not be minimized.

** From Success to Closure

- However, this success is often taken to support a much stronger conclusion.
- Many assume that the success of physics demonstrates, or at least strongly suggests, that physical explanation is in principle complete.
- This assumption takes various forms: that every event has a physical cause, that every phenomenon is reducible to physical processes, that physics will eventually explain everything.
- These commitments constitute the thesis of explanatory closure of the physical.
- Closure is rarely argued for explicitly; it functions as a background metaphysical commitment in much scientific and philosophical discourse.
- The inference from predictive success to explanatory completeness is, however, philosophical rather than scientific.
- The present paper examines whether closure logically follows from scientific success.

** The Target: A Common Argument Against Miracles

- One significant application of explanatory closure is in arguments against the possibility of miracles.
- A common atheist argument runs as follows: miracles would violate natural law; natural law is exceptionless; therefore miracles cannot occur.
- This argument presupposes explanatory closure: it assumes that the domain of physical law exhausts the domain of possible events.
- If closure fails, this argument fails with it.
- Our aim is to show that closure does fail, thereby removing one prominent obstacle to belief in miracles.
- We do not argue that any miracle has occurred; we argue only that physics cannot rule them out in principle.

* Physical Explanation as Derivability

** The Need for a Rigorous Account of Explanation

- Ordinary usage of "explanation" is loose and context-dependent.
- Scientists speak of explaining phenomena, but the standards for explanation vary across contexts.
- For our purposes, we need a rigorous account that captures the strongest sense in which physics aspires to explain.
- A weak notion of explanation would make the closure thesis trivially false; a strong notion is required to give it teeth.
- We therefore adopt the strongest defensible notion: explanation as logical derivation from fundamental principles.

** Derivability from Axioms

- Definition 1 (Physical Explanation): An event E is physically explained if and only if E is derivable from the axioms of physical law together with relevant initial and boundary conditions.
- Symbolically: Let P denote the set of axioms of physical law (e.g., the Schrödinger equation, Einstein field equations, Standard Model Lagrangian) and let I denote the relevant initial and boundary conditions.
- Then E is physically explained iff P ∪ I ⊢ E.
- The turnstile ⊢ denotes derivability in the mathematical sense: there exists a finite sequence of steps, each licensed by the inference rules of the system, terminating in E.
- This is the deductive-nomological model of explanation, associated with Hempel and Oppenheim, in its strongest form.

** Why This Strong Notion?

- One might object that this notion is too strong—that working physicists do not typically produce complete derivations.
- This objection confuses practice with aspiration.
- Working physicists operate with approximations, incomplete knowledge of initial conditions, and computational limitations.
- But the regulative ideal of physics—the goal toward which fundamental physics strives—is precisely a complete theory from which all phenomena are in principle derivable.
- The search for a "theory of everything" embodies this aspiration.
- When physicists say they have explained a phenomenon, they mean (implicitly) that the phenomenon is derivable from fundamental principles, even if the derivation is too complex to carry out in practice.
- Our argument targets this regulative ideal, not the pragmatic shortcuts of working science.

** The Alternative: Phenomenological Description

- Any account of a phenomenon that lacks derivability from fundamental principles is, strictly speaking, phenomenological rather than explanatory.
- Phenomenological models describe regularities without deriving them from deeper principles.
- Thermodynamics before statistical mechanics was largely phenomenological: it described heat phenomena without explaining why matter behaves as it does.
- The transition from phenomenological description to genuine explanation is precisely the transition to derivability from fundamental axioms.
- This distinction is recognized within physics itself and is not an external philosophical imposition.

** Explanation vs. Prediction vs. Description

- It is important to distinguish explanation from prediction and description.
- Description states what happens; prediction states what will happen; explanation states why it happens.
- A successful prediction does not constitute an explanation unless the prediction is derived from explanatory principles.
- Statistical correlations may permit prediction without explanation.
- Machine learning models may achieve high predictive accuracy without providing any explanation of the phenomena they predict.
- Explanation, in the strong sense, requires derivability from fundamental law.

* Physics as a Formal Explanatory System

** The Structural Analogy

- Physical theory has the structure of a formal axiomatic system.
- Axioms: the fundamental laws of physics (field equations, Lagrangians, symmetry principles).
- Inference rules: the rules of mathematical derivation (calculus, differential geometry, algebra, logic).
- Theorems: the derived consequences, i.e., predictions and explanations of phenomena.
- This is not merely an analogy; it is a description of how theoretical physics actually operates.
- Physicists derive consequences from fundamental principles using mathematical reasoning.
- The validity of a derivation is assessed by the same standards that govern formal proof.

** The Formalizability of Physics

- A potential objection: physics is not a formal system in the sense required for Gödelian reasoning.
- Response: any content that can be expressed mathematically can be formalized.
- Physical laws are expressed as differential equations, variational principles, and algebraic structures.
- These mathematical formulations are fully formalizable in standard mathematical logic.
- Indeed, much of mathematical physics can be formalized within Zermelo-Fraenkel set theory.
- If physics were not formalizable, it would not be the precise mathematical science it is.

** Expressivity and Arithmetic

- Gödel's incompleteness theorems apply to formal systems capable of expressing arithmetic.
- More precisely, they apply to systems capable of representing the primitive recursive functions.
- Any physical theory capable of describing computation meets this criterion.
- Modern physics can describe Turing machines: we can give a physical specification of a computer and derive its behaviour from physical law.
- Therefore, the formal system of physics (assuming such a system is consistent) is subject to Gödelian incompleteness.
- This is not a mere analogy; the conditions for Gödel's theorems are met.

** Revisions to the Axioms

- One might object that physical laws are revisable: we add new axioms, modify old ones, change the mathematical framework.
- This is true but does not escape the argument.
- At any given time, physics operates with a set of axioms.
- Revising the axioms amounts to changing the formal system—moving to a different formal system.
- Each such system, if consistent, is incomplete.
- There is no escape by revision: the new system will have its own non-derivable truths.
- The argument does not depend on physics having reached its final form; it applies to any consistent formal system that could serve as the axioms of physical law.

** The Aspiration to a Final Theory

- Some physicists believe a final theory is possible: a complete set of fundamental laws from which all physical phenomena are derivable.
- Weinberg, for example, wrote of "Dreams of a Final Theory."
- Our argument shows this dream is unrealizable, if the final theory is to be both consistent and complete.
- A consistent final theory will have non-derivable truths.
- An inconsistent final theory is not a theory at all—from a contradiction, anything follows, and the theory loses all predictive and explanatory content.

* The Gödelian Argument

** Gödel's First Incompleteness Theorem (Informal Statement)

- Theorem (Gödel, 1931): Any consistent formal system capable of expressing arithmetic is incomplete.
- "Incomplete" means: there exist statements in the language of the system that are true but not provable within the system.
- More precisely: there exists a sentence G such that neither G nor ¬G is derivable from the axioms, yet G is true (in the standard model).
- The proof proceeds by constructing a sentence that "says" of itself that it is not provable—a formalized version of the Liar Paradox.

** The Scope of Incompleteness

- A crucial point: Gödel's theorem does not merely show that one particular self-referential sentence is undecidable.
- It shows that any consistent, sufficiently expressive formal system has undecidable sentences.
- Subsequent work, particularly by Chaitin and in algorithmic information theory, shows that undecidability is pervasive.
- Almost all mathematical truths are not derivable from any given finite axiomatic system.
- The self-referential Gödel sentence is the proof technique, not the boundary of the phenomenon.
- Therefore, the worry that "only weird metamathematical sentences are undecidable" is unfounded.

** Application to Physical Theory

- Let P be the formal system constituting physical law (axioms of fundamental physics, formalized in an appropriate mathematical language).
- Let E be the set of all true event-statements (statements describing events that actually occur or have occurred).
- The thesis of explanatory closure asserts: for all e in E, P ⊢ e.
- That is, every true event-statement is derivable from physical law (together with initial conditions).
- But if P is consistent and sufficiently expressive (which it is, since it can describe computation), then P is incomplete.
- Therefore: there exists some e in E such that P ⊬ e.
- Some true event-statements are not derivable from physical law.

** The Formal Dilemma

- We can state this as a dilemma:
  1. Either P is inconsistent, or
  2. There exists e ∈ E such that P ⊬ e.
- If P is inconsistent, physics collapses: an inconsistent system proves everything and explains nothing.
- Therefore, assuming physics is consistent (as we must, for it to be a useful theory), there exist non-derivable true events.
- Explanatory closure is false.

** What Kind of Events Are Non-Derivable?

- The Gödelian argument establishes existence, not identification.
- We know non-derivable true event-statements exist; we do not know which events they describe.
- They need not be exotic or obviously miraculous.
- They might include highly complex events whose derivation exceeds any finite proof.
- They might include events involving sensitive dependence on aspects of reality not captured by physical law.
- The argument does not allow us to point to a particular event and declare it non-derivable.
- It establishes only that the class of non-derivable events is non-empty.

* The Definition of Supernatural Events

** Nature and Natural Events

- What do we mean by "nature" and "natural"?
- We propose: nature is the domain of lawlike regularity—events that are predictable, orderly, and subsumable under mathematical theory.
- A natural event is one that is derivable from the laws of nature (physical law) together with initial conditions.
- This definition is not arbitrary; it captures the implicit understanding operative in natural science.
- The "natural" is precisely what natural science studies: the lawlike, the regular, the derivable.

** Supernatural Events Defined

- Definition 2 (Supernatural Event): A supernatural event is a true event-statement E such that P ⊬ E.
- That is, a supernatural event is an event that actually occurs but is not derivable from physical law.
- This definition is the natural complement to our definition of natural events.
- An event is supernatural if it lies outside the domain of physical derivation.

** Clarifications

- Supernatural events, so defined, are not logical contradictions.
- A contradiction is derivable from any set of premises (ex falso quodlibet); non-derivability implies non-contradictoriness.
- Supernatural events need not violate the laws of physics.
- Physical laws describe what is derivable; they do not prohibit what is not derivable.
- A non-derivable event does not contradict physical law; it simply falls outside its scope.
- Supernatural events are exceptional, not impossible.

** The Distinction from Law-Violation

- A common conception of miracles is that they violate natural law.
- Our conception is different: supernatural events are not violations but exceptions.
- A violation would be an event that contradicts what physical law predicts—for example, if the law says the ball must fall and the ball rises instead.
- An exception is an event that physical law does not address—an event outside its derivational scope.
- The supernatural, on our account, is not the anti-natural but the extra-natural.

** Why This Definition Is Not Gerrymandered

- One might object that we have defined "supernatural" in a tendentious way to make our argument work.
- Response: our definition captures the historic understanding of the natural/supernatural distinction.
- The "natural" has always meant the domain of regular, lawlike, explicable phenomena.
- The "supernatural" has always meant what lies beyond this domain.
- Our definition precisifies this intuitive distinction; it does not depart from it.
- Any alternative definition that makes "nature" coextensive with "everything that happens" would be stipulative and would eliminate the distinction by fiat.

* Objections and Replies

** Objection 1: No Such Event Has Ever Occurred

- Objection: There is no evidence that any event has ever occurred outside physical law. All observed events have natural explanations.
- Reply: This objection requires total historical knowledge.
- To assert that every event has been explained by physics, one must know every event that has occurred and verify its derivability.
- This is beyond human epistemic capacity.
- Moreover, the objection confuses "not known to be non-derivable" with "known to be derivable."
- Most events have never been derived from physical law; they are simply assumed to be derivable in principle.
- The objection thus presupposes closure rather than arguing for it.

** Objection 2: Future Science Will Explain Everything

- Objection: Even if current physics is incomplete, future physics will achieve complete explanation. We should not infer supernatural events from current gaps.
- Reply: This objection assumes closure in advance.
- It asserts that complete explanation is possible in principle, which is precisely what the Gödelian argument denies.
- The incompleteness is not due to the current state of physics but to the formal nature of any consistent axiomatic system.
- No future physics, however advanced, can escape Gödel's theorem if it remains consistent and sufficiently expressive.
- The objection is therefore a statement of faith in closure, not an argument for it.

** Objection 3: Supernatural Events Violate Natural Law

- Objection: If a supernatural event occurred, it would violate natural law, which is impossible.
- Reply: This objection rests on a confusion between laws as descriptions and laws as necessities.
- Physical laws describe regularities in nature; they do not impose metaphysical necessities.
- A law tells us what happens in the ordinary course of things; it does not preclude exceptions.
- Moreover, as argued above, supernatural events do not violate laws; they fall outside their scope.
- Non-derivability is not contradiction.

** Objection 4: This Undermines Science

- Objection: If we admit supernatural events, we open the door to obscurantism and undermine the scientific enterprise.
- Reply: Science already handles singular events that are not explained by general laws.
- Historical sciences (cosmology, geology, evolutionary biology) study unique events.
- The Big Bang, the formation of the solar system, particular speciation events—these are singular occurrences, not instances of repeatable regularities.
- Acknowledging that some events may lie outside physical derivation does not prevent us from seeking physical explanations where they exist.
- It merely requires intellectual honesty about the limits of physical explanation.

** Objection 5: Gödel's Theorem Does Not Apply to Physics

- Objection: Gödel's theorem applies to formal mathematical systems, not to empirical science. Physics is not mathematics.
- Reply: This objection faces a dilemma.
- If physics is formal enough to be a rigorous mathematical science—if its laws are expressed as equations and its derivations are mathematical proofs—then it is subject to Gödelian limits.
- If physics is not formal enough—if it is merely a loose collection of heuristics and approximations—then it already fails to provide the kind of derivational explanation that closure requires.
- Either way, explanatory closure fails.
- The objector cannot have it both ways: claiming physics is rigorous enough to explain everything but not rigorous enough to be subject to incompleteness.

** Objection 6: Causal Closure Is Distinct from Explanatory Closure

- Objection: Even if physical explanation is incomplete, all events may still have physical causes. Causal closure may hold even if explanatory closure does not.
- Reply: What does "physical cause" mean if not "cause subsumable under physical law"?
- To say A physically causes B means that B follows from A by physical law.
- If B is not derivable from physical law, in what sense is its cause "physical"?
- The objector is making a metaphysical assertion—that all causes are physical—without any criterion for what makes a cause physical.
- This is not a scientific claim but a metaphysical stipulation.
- Asserting "all events have physical causes" without specifying those causes or showing their derivability is an empty promissory note.
- It assumes closure rather than establishing it.

** Objection 7: The Undecidable Statements Are Only Metamathematical

- Objection: Gödel's undecidable sentences are self-referential mathematical statements, not empirical claims about physical events. The argument equivocates between mathematical and physical statements.
- Reply: This objection underestimates the scope of incompleteness.
- First, the Gödel sentence is a proof technique, not the boundary of undecidability. Chaitin's work shows undecidability is pervasive.
- Second, physics describes physical events using mathematical language. Event-statements are mathematical statements within the formalism of physics.
- Third, to assert that the undecidable statements are confined to a domain irrelevant to physics is itself a metaphysical claim—one that cannot be established within physics (for that would require proving something about what physics cannot prove).
- The objection thus assumes what it needs to prove.

* Philosophical and Theological Implications

** The Undermining of Strong Physicalism

- Strong physicalism holds that everything that exists is physical and everything that happens is physically explicable.
- Our argument undermines the explanatory component of this thesis.
- If some true events are not derivable from physical law, then not everything is physically explicable.
- Physicalism may remain a research program or a methodological stance, but it cannot claim the status of established truth.

** The Status of Reductionism

- Reductionism holds that higher-level phenomena (chemistry, biology, psychology) are in principle reducible to physics.
- Reduction means derivability: chemistry reduces to physics if chemical facts are derivable from physical law.
- If physics itself is incomplete, then the reductionist program cannot succeed in full generality.
- Some facts at some level must be non-derivable from physics.
- This does not refute reductionism as a methodological heuristic, but it does refute it as a metaphysical thesis.

** Empirical Science Remains Intact

- Our argument does not threaten the practice of empirical science.
- Science proceeds by seeking explanations; it does not require that explanations exist for everything.
- Scientists can continue to look for physical explanations without assuming that every event has one.
- The difference is between methodological naturalism (we look for natural explanations) and metaphysical naturalism (only natural explanations exist).
- Our argument refutes the latter but is compatible with the former.

** The Logical Space for Miracles

- Our argument opens logical space for miracles.
- A miracle, traditionally understood, is an event brought about by God (or some supernatural agent) that exceeds the powers of nature.
- On our account, such an event would be a true event-statement not derivable from physical law.
- Our argument shows that such events are possible in principle: physics cannot rule them out.
- This does not prove that miracles occur; it removes one argument for thinking they cannot.

** The Symmetry of Metaphysical Commitments

- The atheist who asserts that miracles are impossible because they violate natural law is making a metaphysical claim.
- The theist who asserts that miracles are possible is also making a metaphysical claim.
- Our argument shows that neither claim is scientifically established.
- The atheist cannot appeal to science to rule out miracles in principle.
- The playing field is level: both theism and atheism are metaphysical positions that go beyond what science can establish.
- The atheist must argue for naturalism on philosophical grounds, not claim it as a scientific result.

** Epistemic Humility

- Our argument counsels epistemic humility about the scope of physical explanation.
- We should not assume that physics explains everything merely because it explains much.
- We should not assume that unexplained events will eventually be explained by physics.
- We should remain open to the possibility that some events lie outside the domain of physical law.
- This humility is not obscurantism; it is honest acknowledgment of the limits of our knowledge.

* Conclusion

** Summary of the Argument

- We defined physical explanation as derivability from axioms of physical law.
- We noted that physics, so conceived, constitutes a formal explanatory system.
- We applied Gödel's incompleteness theorem: any consistent, sufficiently expressive formal system is incomplete.
- We concluded that some true event-statements are not derivable from physical law.
- We defined supernatural events as true events not so derivable.
- We therefore concluded that supernatural events are logically possible: physics cannot rule them out in principle.

** What the Argument Does Not Establish

- The argument does not establish that any supernatural event has occurred.
- It does not identify which events, if any, are supernatural.
- It does not characterize what supernatural events would look like.
- It does not prove the existence of God or any supernatural agent.
- It establishes only that the logical space for supernatural events exists and cannot be closed by appeal to physics.

** The Dialectical Achievement

- The argument dismantles a common atheist objection to miracles: that they are ruled out by natural law.
- It shows that explanatory closure is a metaphysical assumption, not a scientific result.
- It places the burden back on the naturalist to argue for naturalism on philosophical grounds.
- It opens the door; it does not walk through it.

** Final Statement

- Physical law describes the regular, the lawlike, the derivable.
- But reality may exceed its lawlike structure.
- There may be true events that no physical theory can derive.
- These events constitute the domain of the supernatural.
- Science cannot close the door on them.
- The possibility of miracles remains.

* Appendix: Technical Details on Incompleteness

** Formal Statement of Gödel's First Incompleteness Theorem

- Let T be a formal system satisfying the following conditions:
  1. T is consistent (no contradiction is derivable from T).
  2. T is recursively axiomatizable (the set of axioms is decidable).
  3. T is sufficiently expressive (T can represent all primitive recursive functions).
- Then there exists a sentence G in the language of T such that:
  - If T is consistent, then T ⊬ G.
  - If T is ω-consistent, then T ⊬ ¬G.
  - G is true in the standard model.

** Why Physical Theory Meets the Conditions

- Consistency: Physical theory is assumed consistent; an inconsistent physics would be useless.
- Recursive axiomatizability: The axioms of physics (field equations, Lagrangians, etc.) are finitely specifiable.
- Sufficient expressivity: Physics can describe Turing machines; any such system can represent primitive recursive functions.
- Therefore, physical theory (when formalized) is subject to Gödel's theorem.

** The Scope Beyond Self-Reference

- Gödel's proof constructs a specific self-referential sentence, but the theorem's implications extend further.
- Chaitin showed that for any formal system T, there is a constant c such that T cannot prove "K(s) > c" for any string s, even though infinitely many such statements are true.
- Here K(s) denotes the Kolmogorov complexity of s.
- This shows that incompleteness is pervasive, not confined to self-referential constructions.
- The non-derivable truths form an infinite, dense class, not a few exotic exceptions.

** Incompleteness of Physical Explanation

*** Formal Dilemma

- Let *P* be the axiomatic system of physical law.
- Let *E* be the set of true event-statements.
- Closure claim:
  ∀e ∈ E, P ⊢ e

*** Incompleteness Consequence

- Either:

  * ∃e ∈ E such that P ⊬ e (incompleteness), or
  * P is inconsistent.
- Therefore:
  ¬(∀e ∈ E, P ⊢ e)

*** Conclusion

- Exhaustive physical explanation is impossible in principle.

## Abstract

The extraordinary predictive success of modern physics has encouraged a widespread metaphysical thesis: that all real events are, in principle, fully explicable by physical law. This thesis—often termed explanatory or causal closure of the physical—is rarely defended but frequently assumed. This paper challenges closure by treating physical explanation as formal derivability from axioms of physical law. Drawing on incompleteness-style reasoning and the empirical nature of scientific theory, it is argued that no sufficiently expressive system of physical laws can be explanatorily complete. Consequently, some true event-statements must lie outside physical derivation. This establishes logical space for real non-derivable events, here termed supernatural events, without undermining the legitimacy of scientific practice.

---

## 1. Introduction

Since the scientific revolution, natural phenomena have increasingly been explained through mathematical laws. Classical mechanics, thermodynamics, quantum theory, and general relativity collectively provide models of astonishing precision and scope. From this success has emerged a philosophical position often left implicit: that reality is exhaustively governed by physical law and that every true event is, in principle, derivable from physical theory.

This assumption underlies much contemporary naturalism, physicalism, and reductionism. It is often expressed informally as the belief that “everything that happens has a physical explanation,” or more formally as the causal closure of the physical domain.

Yet this thesis is rarely examined as a claim about the structure of explanation itself. This paper argues that once physical explanation is understood rigorously as derivation from axioms of physical law, closure becomes impossible in principle. The limitation is not technological or epistemic but formal.

---

## 2. Physical Law and the Ideal of Derivation

Let us begin by clarifying what it would mean for physics to fully explain an event.

### Definition (Strong Physical Explanation):

An event *E* is physically explained if *E* is derivable from the axioms of physical law together with relevant initial conditions.

This conception aligns with the traditional reductionist ideal in theoretical physics: that all macroscopic phenomena ultimately follow from fundamental dynamics.

Under this framework, physical theory constitutes a formal system:

* **Axioms**: fundamental laws of nature (e.g., field equations, conservation laws)
* **Inference rules**: mathematical reasoning and dynamical evolution
* **Theorems**: event-statements describing what occurs

Anything less—statistical correlation, curve fitting, phenomenological modelling—falls short of explanation in the strict sense.

If closure is to hold, then every true event-statement must be derivable within this formal structure.

---

## 3. Empirical Science and the Limits of Model-Based Explanation

In practice, scientific laws arise from observed regularities. They are inferred from data, not deduced from first principles. Even the most fundamental theories are accepted because they work, not because they are logically necessary.

Historically, many laws have been superseded or radically revised:

* Newtonian mechanics replaced by relativity
* classical determinism by quantum indeterminacy
* continuous fields by quantised interactions

This reveals an important feature: physical law is not a closed deductive system but an evolving empirical framework.

Consequently, the assumption that such a framework must be capable of deriving every true event is not itself an empirical discovery. It is a philosophical extrapolation from predictive success.

---

## 4. Incompleteness and Formal Systems

In 1931, Gödel demonstrated that any sufficiently expressive formal axiomatic system capable of encoding arithmetic cannot be both complete and consistent (Gödel, 1931).

That is:

* if the system is consistent, there exist true statements not derivable within it
* if the system is complete, it must contain contradictions

While Gödel’s theorems apply strictly to formal mathematical systems, the relevance here lies in structure rather than domain.

If physical theory is treated as a formal explanatory system intended to generate all true event-statements as consequences of its axioms, then incompleteness pressure arises.

Any system expressive enough to encode rich causal structure—something physics must do—cannot guarantee complete derivability of all truths.

---

## 5. The Incompleteness of Physical Explanation

From the preceding sections, we face a fundamental dilemma:

### Either:

1. The formal system of physical law is incomplete.
   Some true event-statements are not derivable from physical axioms.

### Or:

2. The system is inconsistent.
   The axioms fail to hold universally.

In both cases:

> Not all true events are physically explicable.

Thus strong explanatory closure is impossible in principle.

If physics is formal enough to truly explain, incompleteness follows.

If it is not formal enough, it never achieves complete explanation.

Either way, exhaustive physical derivation collapses.

---

## 6. Observation Versus Derivation

Much of human knowledge rests on observation and testimony rather than theoretical derivation.

Historical events—wars, discoveries, catastrophes—are not deduced from laws of nature. They are known because they occurred and were recorded.

Science itself often encounters phenomena before possessing explanatory frameworks (e.g., superconductivity, dark energy, quantum tunnelling).

Truth is not conditional on derivability.

An event may be real and rationally known even in the absence of physical explanation.

---

## 7. Supernatural Events as Non-Derivable Events

Let us define minimally:

> **Supernatural Event**: a true event-statement not derivable from physical law.

This definition avoids sensationalism and focuses purely on explanatory status.

Given the formal incompleteness of physical explanation, the existence of such events is not merely possible but unavoidable in principle—assuming consistency of the system.

This does not imply frequency, predictability, or theological interpretation.

It establishes only that physical law cannot exhaust reality.

---

## 8. Common Objections

### 8.1 “No event has ever occurred outside physical law.”

This claim is not empirically verifiable.

It would require complete knowledge of:

* all past events
* their full derivability from physical axioms

At best, we can observe that many phenomena conform to regularities.

The universal claim of closure is metaphysical, not scientific.

---

### 8.2 “Future science will explain everything.”

This assumes explanatory completeness in advance.

It is a commitment to physicalism, not an inference from evidence.

Incompleteness reasoning shows that even perfect science cannot guarantee full derivation.

---

### 8.3 “Supernatural Events violate laws of nature.”

Physical laws describe regular behaviour under ordinary conditions.

They are not logical necessities.

Additional causes do not logically contradict regularities (as technological intervention routinely demonstrates).

---

### 8.4 “Allowing supernatural events undermines science.”

Science already accommodates singular events:

* cosmological origins
* mass extinctions
* evolutionary contingencies

Regularity enables prediction; it does not require metaphysical closure.

---

### 8.5 “Gödel does not apply to physics.”

If physics is formal enough for derivation, incompleteness applies.

If it is not, closure already fails.

Either horn defeats exhaustive physical explanation.

---

## 9. Philosophical Implications

The argument undermines:

* strong physicalism
* explanatory reductionism
* metaphysical naturalism grounded in closure

while preserving:

* empirical science
* methodological naturalism
* predictive modelling

It restores the distinction between regularities and reality itself.

---

## 10. Conclusion

The success of physical science does not entail its explanatory completeness.

If explanation requires derivation, incompleteness ensures non-derivable truths.

If physics is merely empirical modelling, closure fails outright.

Either way, reality cannot be exhaustively captured by physical law.

The logical space for real non-derivable events—supernatural events—remains unavoidable.

Science neither proves nor disproves them.

It simply does not govern the totality of what may occur.
